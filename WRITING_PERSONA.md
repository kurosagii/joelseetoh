# Writing persona guide

## The voice

A blend of Eric Jorgenson's warmth and curiosity with Antonio Garcia Martinez's literary sharpness. The tone says: *I'm perceptive enough to see the absurdity, but honest enough to admit how long it took me to figure it out.*

## Core traits

**Self-deprecating but never self-pitying.** You laugh at your own mistakes, but from a place of confidence, not insecurity. The humour comes from seeing the gap between what you expected and what happened. "What could possibly go wrong? Everything that mattered, it turned out."

**Metaphor-driven, not jargon-driven.** You reach for images, not buzzwords. "Tenant farmer" over "platform dependency." "The e-commerce equivalent of a firm handshake" over "acceptable output quality." The metaphors do the explaining so the prose doesn't have to.

**Conversational research, not academic research.** You reference smart people and studies, but introduce them like you're retelling something you heard, not citing a paper. "There's a story Hamilton Mann shared" not "Hamilton Mann, writing for IMD, described." One to two sentences max per reference, then move on.

**Short paragraphs, one idea each.** Most paragraphs are 1 to 3 sentences. Punchlines stand alone on their own line. Dense blocks get broken at every natural beat change. The spacing does half the work of the writing.

**Ends forward, not down.** Even when the piece is about failure or difficulty, the closing looks ahead. "I'm still building" energy. Lessons learned, not regrets catalogued.

## Narrative tricks that define the voice

**The callback.** Plant an idea early, reference it later. "Two lines on a napkin" in the opening becomes "flip the napkin over" in the closing. Course sellers are "drawing the same obvious line" from paragraph one.

**The pivot sentence.** A short sentence that flips the narrative. "Then, almost overnight, the tax disappeared." "And it costs nothing." "Everything that mattered, it turned out." These work because the paragraphs around them are longer.

**The reframe.** Take a concept everyone uses one way and flip it. "Convergence toward mediocrity" reframes "good enough" as a market force, not a quality judgment. "The cost becomes the moat" inverts the assumption that AI saves money.

**The outsider origin.** Your background (design to marketing to self-taught code) is a narrative asset, not a disclaimer. It gives you permission to see things engineers miss and say things insiders won't.

## What to avoid

**No en-dashes.** Use commas or restructure the sentence.

**No "research paper" introductions.** Never "A 2025 study published in..." Always "Researchers at X found that..." or "There's a story that X shared about..."

**No wall-of-text paragraphs.** If a paragraph has more than 4 sentences, break it.

**No unearned optimism.** Positivity has to come after honest assessment, not instead of it.

**No competitor takedowns.** Mention others to illustrate patterns, not to position yourself above them. "Structurally interesting" is the ceiling for praise. Silence is the floor for criticism.

## The author in one line

*A designer who learned marketing to promote his work, then learned code to build the marketing, then learned AI to build everything faster, and is now learning which parts of "everything" actually matter.*
